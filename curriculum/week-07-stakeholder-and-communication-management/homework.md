# Week 7 — Homework

Five problems, ~4 hours total, spread across the week. These reinforce the lectures with a mix of applied writing, structured analysis, and one interview-style reflection. Commit each.

All problems reference Project Atlas and Northlight Software unless a problem says otherwise (see [Lecture 1](./lecture-notes/01-stakeholder-mapping.md) for the full cast).

---

## Problem 1 — Map a real project's stakeholders (60 min)

In `real-stakeholder-map.md`, pick a real group project you've been part of — school, work, volunteer, a club, even organizing a family event — and identify **5–7 real stakeholders**. For each: score power and interest (1–5, with a one-sentence justification each), place them in a quadrant, and note whether, looking back, you actually engaged them the way their quadrant suggests you should have. Where you didn't, what actually happened as a result — did a "Keep Satisfied" person get surprised late? Did a "Keep Informed" person go around you because they felt ignored? Be specific and honest.

---

## Problem 2 — Rewrite three real vague or buried status updates (45 min)

Find (or reconstruct from memory) **three real status updates** you've received or written — an email, a Slack message, a standup summary — that either buried bad news or were too vague to act on. In `status-rewrites.md`, for each: quote or paraphrase the original, name specifically what was buried or vague, and rewrite it using Lecture 2's one-glance format (§4) and the "don't bury a slip" discipline (§5).

---

## Problem 3 — Interview someone about a stakeholder conflict, or analyze one from a distance (60 min)

Pick **one**:

- **(A)** Ask someone with real project or team-lead experience: "Tell me about a time two people with power wanted opposite things on a project you were running, and how you got to a decision." Write up their answer and one thing that surprised you in `interview.md`.
- **(B)** If you can't find someone to interview, find a **public example** of a stakeholder conflict in a well-known project or product decision (a public postmortem, a well-documented product pivot, a widely reported internal disagreement at a company) and analyze it through Lecture 3, §5's four-step lens: were positions separated from underlying needs? Were facts presented neutrally? Was the trade-off named explicitly? Was there a real, documented decision? Write this in `conflict-analysis.md` (250–400 words), citing your source.

---

## Problem 4 — Escalation vs. complaint, in your own history (30 min)

In `escalation-reflection.md` (200–300 words): think of a time you raised a problem to someone with more authority than you (at work, school, or elsewhere). Using Lecture 3, §1–2's four-part template, was your original message closer to a complaint or a real escalation? Rewrite it now as a proper four-part escalation, and reflect on whether you think it would have gotten a faster or clearer response in that form.

---

## Problem 5 — Design a communication plan for something absurdly small (60 min)

Communication planning isn't just for big projects — the discipline scales down. In `tiny-comms-plan.md`, pick something small and concrete you're actually organizing or could organize (a study group, a small event, a household project with roommates or family, a small open-source contribution) and identify 3–4 real stakeholders. For each, write one real communication-plan row: message focus, channel, cadence, and one sentence on why that combination fits that person. The goal is proving the discipline works at any scale, not just for eight-stakeholder company initiatives.
