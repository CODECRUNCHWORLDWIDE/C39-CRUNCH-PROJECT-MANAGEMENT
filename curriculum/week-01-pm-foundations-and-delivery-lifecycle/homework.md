# Week 1 — Homework

Five problems, ~4–5 hours total, spread across the week. These reinforce the lectures with a mix of applied writing, structured analysis, and one interview-style reflection. Commit each.

All problems reference Project Atlas and Northlight Software unless a problem says otherwise (see [Lecture 1](./lecture-notes/01-what-a-pm-does.md) for the full cast).

---

## Problem 1 — Interview a PM, or diagnose one from a distance (60 min)

Real project management is learned partly by watching it happen. Pick **one**:

- **(A)** Find someone in your network who has run projects (a PM, an EM, a tech lead, even an informal one) and ask them: "Tell me about a project that went badly because scope wasn't clear up front" and "How do you decide when to escalate a problem versus handling it yourself?" Write up their answers and one thing that surprised you, in `interview.md`.
- **(B)** If you can't find someone to interview, find a **public postmortem** of a failed or troubled software project (search "[company name] postmortem" or "incident retrospective") and analyze it through this week's lens instead: which lifecycle phase (Lecture 2) was skipped or rushed, and what would a proper charter (Lecture 3) have prevented? Write this analysis in `postmortem-analysis.md` (250–400 words), citing the source.

---

## Problem 2 — Map your own work history to the lifecycle (45 min)

In `my-lifecycle-example.md`, describe one real project you've been part of (school, work, volunteer, personal — anything with a real beginning and end). For each of the five lifecycle phases (Lecture 2), write 2–3 sentences: did that phase happen explicitly, happen informally, or get skipped entirely? If a phase was skipped, what actually went wrong later as a direct result (be specific and honest — "nothing went wrong" is an acceptable answer only if it's true, but most real projects have at least one rough edge traceable to a skipped or rushed phase).

---

## Problem 3 — Rewrite three real vague requests (45 min)

Outside of this week's exercises, find (or invent, based on real patterns you've seen) **three vague requests** you've actually heard in a work, school, or team context — things like "make it more user-friendly" or "we need this done ASAP." In `vague-to-precise.md`, for each: quote the original request, then rewrite it as a measurable success criterion (Lecture 3, §2) the way you did in Exercise 3, plus one sentence on who you'd need to confirm the interpretation with in real life.

---

## Problem 4 — The triple constraint in your own words (30 min)

In `triple-constraint-reflection.md` (200–300 words), answer: think of a time (any context — work, school, a personal project, even something non-technical like planning an event) where scope, time, or cost got pushed without anyone explicitly deciding what would give. What actually happened to quality, and was it a visible, chosen trade-off or a silent one (Lecture 3, §3)? What would you do differently now, having read this week's material?

---

## Problem 5 — Draft a charter for something absurdly small (60 min)

Charters aren't just for big initiatives — the discipline of writing one scales down. In `tiny-charter.md`, write a **genuinely one-paragraph** charter (not the full mini-project format — a compressed version, 5–8 sentences total covering objective, one success criterion, one in-scope/out-of-scope boundary, and one constraint) for something small and concrete: organizing a study group, planning a weekend trip, running a small event, cleaning up a messy codebase's test suite — anything real and modest in scope. The goal is proving the charter habit works at any size, not just for six-week team initiatives.
